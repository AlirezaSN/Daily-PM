# Why Product Strategy Matters

Imagine you join a company where every week a different stakeholder requests a new feature.

* Marketing wants a referral program.
* Sales wants enterprise reporting.
* Engineering wants to rewrite the backend.
* The CEO wants AI.
* Customers request dozens of new features.

Without a strategy, every request feels equally important.

The roadmap becomes chaotic.

Resources are spread too thin.

Eventually, the product loses focus.

A product strategy provides a clear direction for deciding **what to build—and what not to build**.

---

# What Is Product Strategy?

A **Product Strategy** is a high-level plan that explains **how a product will achieve its vision while supporting business goals**.

It answers questions like:

* Which customers will we serve?
* Which problems will we solve?
* How will we differentiate from competitors?
* Where will we invest?
* Where will we *not* invest?

Think of strategy as a set of choices—not a list of features.

---

# Vision vs Strategy vs Roadmap

These three concepts are often confused.

## Product Vision

The long-term future you want to create.

Usually lasts for many years.

Example:

> "Create the world's most trusted professional network."

Vision rarely changes.

---

## Product Strategy

The plan for achieving that vision.

Usually evolves every 1–3 years.

Example:

* Focus on creators.
* Improve job matching.
* Expand AI-powered recruiting.

Strategy changes as markets change.

---

## Product Roadmap

The planned initiatives over the coming months.

Examples:

* Launch AI resume review.
* Improve messaging.
* Build recruiter dashboard.

Roadmaps change frequently.

---

## Relationship

```text id="vsr1"
Vision
   ↓
Strategy
   ↓
Roadmap
   ↓
Features
```

Every feature should support the roadmap.

Every roadmap initiative should support the strategy.

Every strategy should support the vision.

---

# Product Strategy Is About Making Choices

One of the hardest responsibilities of a Product Manager is saying **No**.

Resources are limited.

Time is limited.

Engineering capacity is limited.

You cannot build everything.

Strategy determines:

* Which customers matter most.
* Which opportunities deserve investment.
* Which ideas should wait.

If your roadmap contains everything everyone asked for, you probably don't have a strategy.

---

# The Four Components of Product Strategy

A simple framework consists of four elements:

## 1. Vision

Where are we going?

---

## 2. Target Customers

Who are we serving?

Example:

* Students
* Small businesses
* Enterprise companies
* Developers

Trying to serve everyone often results in serving no one well.

---

## 3. Value Proposition

Why should customers choose us instead of competitors?

Examples:

* Lowest price
* Fastest delivery
* Best user experience
* Highest security
* AI-powered automation

---

## 4. Strategic Initiatives

The major investments that move the product toward its vision.

Examples:

* Improve onboarding.
* Expand internationally.
* Increase personalization.
* Build enterprise capabilities.

---

# Strategic Thinking

Product Managers should constantly ask:

* What market are we competing in?
* What customer problems matter most?
* Where can we win?
* What should we ignore?

Notice that none of these questions mention features.

---

# Good Strategy vs Bad Strategy

## Bad Strategy

"We will build AI."

This is not a strategy.

It is a technology choice.

---

## Better Strategy

"We will reduce job application effort by 50% using AI-powered assistance."

Now we know:

* The customer
* The problem
* The desired outcome
* The role of AI

---

# Strategy Is Not a Feature List

Many companies confuse strategy with a roadmap.

Example:

Bad strategy:

* Build Chat
* Build Notifications
* Build Search
* Build AI

This is simply a backlog.

---

Better strategy:

* Increase user engagement.
* Improve retention.
* Expand into enterprise customers.

Features are selected only after the strategy is clear.

---

# Example: LinkedIn

Imagine you are the VP of Product.

Vision:

> Create economic opportunity for every professional.

Possible strategy:

* Become the best place to discover career opportunities.
* Invest heavily in AI-powered recruiting.
* Improve professional content creation.
* Increase recruiter efficiency.

Roadmap:

* AI resume coach
* Better job recommendations
* Creator analytics
* Recruiter automation

Notice how each level becomes more specific.

---

# Choosing What NOT to Build

One of the most underrated PM skills is saying **No**.

Imagine these requests arrive:

* Cryptocurrency integration
* Gaming features
* Dating functionality
* Music streaming

Could they all be built?

Yes.

Should they?

Probably not.

None align with LinkedIn's strategic direction.

Every "Yes" consumes time, money, and focus.

Strong strategies eliminate distractions.

---

# Strategic Trade-offs

Every strategic decision has an opportunity cost.

Suppose your team can only complete one initiative this quarter:

Option A:

Improve onboarding.

Option B:

Expand into a new country.

Option C:

Reduce infrastructure costs.

Which is best?

There is no universal answer.

It depends on:

* Company goals
* Market conditions
* Customer needs
* Business model
* Current product stage

This is why Product Management has no single "correct" answer.

---

# Competitive Strategy

Product Managers should understand competitors—but not copy them blindly.

Questions to ask:

* Who are our competitors?
* What are they doing well?
* Where are they weak?
* Where can we differentiate?

Winning rarely means copying every feature.

It usually means solving customer problems better.

---

# Strategic Metrics

Every strategy should have measurable outcomes.

Examples:

Strategy:

Increase user engagement.

Possible metrics:

* DAU
* Session length
* Retention

---

Strategy:

Grow enterprise business.

Possible metrics:

* Enterprise ARR
* Customer retention
* Number of enterprise customers

---

Strategy:

Improve onboarding.

Possible metrics:

* Activation rate
* Time-to-first-value
* First-week retention

A strategy without measurable outcomes is only an idea.

---

# Common Strategic Mistakes

## Mistake #1

Trying to serve everyone.

Focus creates competitive advantage.

---

## Mistake #2

Confusing features with strategy.

Features are tools—not the strategy itself.

---

## Mistake #3

Copying competitors.

Build for your customers, not theirs.

---

## Mistake #4

Changing direction every quarter.

Frequent strategic changes confuse teams and customers.

---

## Mistake #5

Ignoring business goals.

Great products create value for both customers and the company.

---

# Interview Question

### Question

What is the difference between Product Vision and Product Strategy?

### Strong Answer

Product Vision describes the long-term future the company wants to create. It is aspirational and relatively stable.

Product Strategy explains how the company will achieve that vision by making deliberate choices about target customers, value propositions, priorities, and investments. Strategy evolves as markets and customer needs change.

---

# Mini Case Study

Imagine you are the PM for a food delivery app.

Your company wants to grow.

You have four possible strategies:

* Expand into more cities.
* Improve delivery speed.
* Focus on grocery delivery.
* Become the lowest-cost option.

You cannot pursue all four effectively.

Which would you choose?

Before answering, consider:

* Customer needs
* Competitive landscape
* Business capabilities
* Long-term differentiation

This exercise demonstrates that strategy is fundamentally about making trade-offs.

---

# Key Takeaways

1. Product Vision defines where you want to go.
2. Product Strategy defines how you will get there.
3. Product Roadmaps define what you plan to build next.
4. Strategy is about making choices.
5. Every "Yes" requires many "No" decisions.
6. Great strategies focus on customer problems—not features.
7. Every strategy should have measurable outcomes.

# PM Reflection

Think about the product you currently work on or know best.

Ask yourself:

* Can you clearly describe its product vision in one sentence?
* What is the company's strategy?
* What initiatives support that strategy?
* Are there roadmap items that don't align with the strategy?
* If your engineering capacity were cut in half, what would you stop building?

If these questions are difficult to answer, the product may lack a clear strategy—or the strategy may not be well communicated.
