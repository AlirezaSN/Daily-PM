# Why Customer Interviews Matter

Imagine your analytics show:

* User retention dropped by 15%.
* Checkout abandonment increased.
* Feature adoption is low.

Analytics tell you **what** happened.

Customer interviews help you understand **why** it happened.

Without talking to users, Product Managers often solve the wrong problem.

---

# What Is a Customer Interview?

A customer interview is a structured conversation designed to understand:

* Customer goals
* Behaviors
* Motivations
* Pain points
* Current workflows
* Decision-making process

The objective is **learning**, not selling or validating your own ideas.

---

# When Should You Conduct Interviews?

Customer interviews are useful throughout the product lifecycle.

## Discovery

Understand customer problems.

---

## Validation

Test assumptions before development.

---

## Prototype Testing

Evaluate early designs.

---

## After Launch

Understand adoption and satisfaction.

---

## Continuous Discovery

Maintain an ongoing understanding of customer needs.

Great Product Managers don't interview customers once—they build a habit of continuous learning.

---

# Preparing for an Interview

Preparation determines interview quality.

Before every interview, answer these questions:

1. What do we want to learn?
2. Who should we interview?
3. How many interviews do we need?
4. Which assumptions are we testing?

Example objective:

> Understand why job seekers abandon applications before submission.

A clear objective keeps the conversation focused.

---

# Choosing the Right Participants

Interview people who actually experience the problem.

Examples:

Good participants:

* Active users
* New users
* Users who recently churned
* Heavy users
* Occasional users

Avoid interviewing only your most loyal customers.

Different customer segments often reveal different problems.

---

# Structure of a Great Interview

A typical customer interview has five stages.

```text id="ci1"
Introduction
      ↓
Background
      ↓
Current Behavior
      ↓
Problems
      ↓
Wrap-up
```

Each stage has a different purpose.

---

# Stage 1: Introduction

Build trust.

Explain:

* Who you are.
* Why you're conducting the interview.
* There are no right or wrong answers.
* You're interested in learning—not judging.

Example:

> "Thank you for joining today. We're trying to understand your experience so we can improve the product. Please be honest—positive and negative feedback are equally valuable."

---

# Stage 2: Understand the Customer

Before discussing the product, learn about the customer.

Examples:

* Tell me about your role.
* How often do you use our product?
* What are you trying to accomplish?
* Walk me through your typical day.

This creates context for later answers.

---

# Stage 3: Explore Current Behavior

Ask about actual experiences—not opinions.

Good questions:

* Tell me about the last time you used the product.
* Walk me through what happened.
* What were you trying to accomplish?
* What worked well?
* What was frustrating?

Past behavior is usually more reliable than future predictions.

---

# Stage 4: Identify Problems

Now investigate pain points.

Examples:

* What was the most difficult part?
* What took longer than expected?
* What surprised you?
* What almost caused you to stop?
* How did you solve it?

Continue asking follow-up questions.

Often the first answer is only the surface problem.

---

# Stage 5: Wrap Up

Finish with broad questions.

Examples:

* Is there anything we didn't discuss?
* If you could change one thing, what would it be?
* What advice would you give our product team?

Many valuable insights appear in the final minutes.

---

# Open vs Closed Questions

## Closed Questions

Usually answered with:

* Yes
* No
* A short response

Example:

> Do you like Dark Mode?

Not very useful.

---

## Open Questions

Encourage storytelling.

Example:

> Tell me about the last time you used the app at night.

Much richer.

Prefer open-ended questions whenever possible.

---

# Good Questions vs Bad Questions

## Bad

> Would you use AI if we added it?

Problems:

* Hypothetical
* Leading
* Focused on the solution

---

## Better

> Tell me about the last time completing this task was difficult.

Now you're learning about the problem.

---

## Bad

> Do you think Feature X is useful?

---

## Better

> Walk me through how you currently accomplish this task.

Behavior is more reliable than opinions.

---

# The "Five Whys"

When users mention a problem, don't stop at the first answer.

Example:

"I couldn't finish the application."

Why?

"The form was too long."

Why?

"I had to re-enter information."

Why?

"My resume wasn't imported."

Eventually, you reach the root cause.

---

# Active Listening

Great interviewers talk less than customers.

Practice:

* Let users finish speaking.
* Don't interrupt.
* Allow silence.
* Ask follow-up questions.
* Repeat important points for confirmation.

Example:

> "If I understood correctly, your biggest frustration is having to enter the same information multiple times. Is that right?"

---

# Interview Notes

Capture:

* Quotes
* Behaviors
* Emotions
* Pain points
* Unexpected observations

Separate **facts** from **interpretations**.

Fact:

> User clicked "Back" three times.

Interpretation:

> User was confused.

Always record the fact first.

---

# Turning Interviews into Insights

After interviewing multiple customers, look for patterns.

Example:

Out of 12 interviews:

* 9 users struggled with onboarding.
* 8 users mentioned missing salary information.
* 7 users abandoned long forms.

Patterns matter more than isolated comments.

---

# Common Interview Mistakes

## Mistake #1

Selling the product during the interview.

You're there to learn—not persuade.

---

## Mistake #2

Asking leading questions.

Bad:

> "Wouldn't this feature help?"

---

## Mistake #3

Talking too much.

A useful guideline:

The customer should speak about **80%** of the time.

---

## Mistake #4

Accepting the first answer.

Keep asking:

> Why?

---

## Mistake #5

Interviewing only one customer type.

Different segments reveal different opportunities.

---

# Interview Example

Imagine you're a PM at LinkedIn.

Objective:

Understand why users don't complete job applications.

Poor Question:

> Would you use an AI resume writer?

Better Questions:

* Tell me about the last job you applied for.
* What steps did you take?
* Which part was most frustrating?
* What almost caused you to stop?
* How did you finish the application?

Notice that none of these questions suggest a solution.

---

# From Interview to Product Decision

Interview Finding:

Users repeatedly abandon applications because they must manually upload resumes for every application.

Possible Solutions:

* Resume autofill
* Saved profiles
* One-click application
* Integration with cloud storage

The interview identifies the problem.

The Product Team evaluates possible solutions.

---

# Interview Checklist

Before the Interview:

* Define your objective.
* Select the right participants.
* Prepare open-ended questions.
* Test your interview guide.

During the Interview:

* Listen actively.
* Avoid leading questions.
* Explore behaviors.
* Ask follow-up questions.

After the Interview:

* Organize notes.
* Look for patterns.
* Validate findings with additional evidence.
* Share insights with the team.

---

# Interview Question

### Question

Why are open-ended questions better than closed questions during customer interviews?

### Strong Answer

Open-ended questions encourage customers to describe real experiences, behaviors, and motivations in their own words. This produces richer insights than closed questions, which often lead to short answers and can unintentionally bias the conversation.

---

# Mini Case Study

You are the PM for a food delivery app.

Many users stop before placing an order.

Your task is to design a customer interview.

Write five questions.

Possible examples:

1. Tell me about the last time you ordered food using our app.
2. What were you trying to accomplish?
3. Was there any point where you felt frustrated?
4. Did you consider leaving the app? Why?
5. If you could improve one part of the ordering experience, what would it be?

Notice that none of these questions mention a specific feature.

---

# Key Takeaways

1. Customer interviews explain **why** users behave the way they do.
2. Interviews should begin with problems—not solutions.
3. Ask open-ended questions whenever possible.
4. Explore actual behavior rather than hypothetical opinions.
5. Listen more than you speak.
6. Look for patterns across multiple interviews.
7. Interviews should inform product decisions—not confirm assumptions.

# PM Reflection

Think about the last meeting where someone said:

> "Customers want Feature X."

Ask yourself:

* Did anyone actually interview customers?
* Were customers describing a problem or proposing a solution?
* What questions would you ask to understand the real need?
* What evidence would you collect before adding the feature to the roadmap?

The strongest Product Managers are curious. They don't assume they know the answer—they investigate until they understand the customer's reality.
