# Day 3: Product Development Lifecycle

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you should understand:

* What the Product Development Lifecycle (PDLC) is
* The major stages of a product's journey
* How ideas become successful products
* The role of a Product Manager in each stage
* Common mistakes organizations make throughout the lifecycle

---

# Why Product Development Lifecycle Matters

Many people think product development begins when developers start coding.

In reality, coding is only one part of a much larger process.

A successful product moves through multiple stages:

1. Discovery
2. Validation
3. Planning
4. Development
5. Launch
6. Growth
7. Maturity
8. Decline or Retirement

Understanding this lifecycle helps PMs make better decisions at every stage.

---

# What Is Product Development Lifecycle?

The Product Development Lifecycle (PDLC) is the end-to-end process of creating, launching, growing, and eventually retiring a product.

A simplified view:

```text id="pdlc1"
Idea
 ↓
Discovery
 ↓
Validation
 ↓
Planning
 ↓
Development
 ↓
Launch
 ↓
Growth
 ↓
Maturity
 ↓
Retirement
```

Every successful product follows some version of this journey.

---

# Stage 1: Discovery

## Purpose

Understand problems before building solutions.

This is where great PMs spend significant time.

---

## Questions Asked

* What problem exists?
* Who experiences this problem?
* How painful is the problem?
* How are people solving it today?

---

## Activities

* Customer interviews
* Surveys
* Market research
* Competitor analysis
* User observation
* Analytics review

---

## Example

Suppose users of a navigation app complain:

> "I don't know which subway route is fastest."

The PM identifies a transportation planning problem.

No solution has been chosen yet.

---

## Common Mistake

Jumping directly to solutions.

Bad:

> Let's build public transportation support.

Good:

> Let's understand the transportation problem first.

---

# Stage 2: Validation

## Purpose

Verify that the problem is important enough to solve.

Not every problem deserves investment.

---

## Questions Asked

* Is the problem real?
* How many users experience it?
* Is solving it valuable?
* Will users adopt a solution?

---

## Activities

* Landing pages
* Prototypes
* User testing
* Smoke tests
* MVP experiments

---

## Example

Before building public transportation support:

* Create clickable prototypes
* Test with users
* Measure interest

If users show little interest, stop early.

---

## Common Mistake

Building before validating.

Many products fail because they solve problems nobody cares about.

---

# Stage 3: Planning

## Purpose

Define what should be built.

Once a problem is validated, planning begins.

---

## Questions Asked

* What should be included?
* What should be excluded?
* What is the MVP?
* What resources are needed?

---

## Activities

* Roadmap creation
* Prioritization
* User stories
* Success metrics
* Scope definition

---

## Deliverables

* Product roadmap
* MVP definition
* Success metrics
* Initial backlog

---

## Common Mistake

Trying to build everything at once.

Good PMs focus on the smallest solution that creates value.

---

# Stage 4: Development

## Purpose

Build the solution.

This is where engineering and design invest most of their effort.

---

## Activities

* Design
* Coding
* Testing
* Sprint planning
* Backlog refinement

---

## PM Responsibilities

* Clarify requirements
* Remove ambiguity
* Prioritize trade-offs
* Answer team questions

---

## Common Mistake

Constantly changing requirements.

Frequent scope changes slow delivery and frustrate teams.

---

# Stage 5: Launch

## Purpose

Release the product to users.

A launch is not the finish line.

It is the beginning of learning.

---

## Activities

* Release planning
* Marketing campaigns
* Internal training
* Customer communication
* Monitoring

---

## Questions Asked

* Are users adopting the feature?
* Are there bugs?
* Is performance acceptable?

---

## Common Mistake

Assuming launch equals success.

Many products launch successfully but fail to gain adoption.

---

# Stage 6: Growth

## Purpose

Increase product adoption and value.

After launch, attention shifts toward growth.

---

## Questions Asked

* How do we attract more users?
* How do we increase engagement?
* How do we improve retention?

---

## Common Metrics

* Daily Active Users (DAU)
* Monthly Active Users (MAU)
* Retention Rate
* Conversion Rate
* Revenue

---

## Example

For LinkedIn:

Growth activities might include:

* Better recommendations
* Improved networking features
* More relevant content

---

## Common Mistake

Focusing only on acquisition while ignoring retention.

Retaining users is often more valuable than acquiring new ones.

---

# Stage 7: Maturity

## Purpose

Maximize efficiency and profitability.

The product is now established.

Growth may slow down.

---

## Characteristics

* Stable customer base
* Predictable revenue
* Incremental improvements
* Increased competition

---

## PM Focus

* Optimization
* Cost management
* Customer retention
* New opportunities

---

## Example

Many enterprise SaaS products spend years in maturity.

---

# Stage 8: Decline or Retirement

## Purpose

Decide whether to continue, transform, or retire the product.

No product lasts forever.

---

## Reasons for Decline

* Market changes
* New technology
* Better competitors
* Customer needs evolve

---

## PM Questions

* Should we invest further?
* Should we pivot?
* Should we retire the product?

---

## Example

Many desktop software products were retired after cloud solutions became dominant.

---

# Product Lifecycle vs Product Development Lifecycle

These concepts are often confused.

## Product Development Lifecycle

Focuses on creating and improving a product.

```text id="pdlc2"
Discovery → Validation → Development → Launch
```

---

## Product Lifecycle

Focuses on market performance.

```text id="plc1"
Introduction → Growth → Maturity → Decline
```

---

# Example: Building a Public Transportation Feature

## Discovery

Users struggle with public transportation planning.

---

## Validation

Research confirms strong demand.

---

## Planning

Define MVP:

* Route planning
* Arrival times
* Station information

Exclude:

* Ticket purchasing
* Bike sharing integration

---

## Development

Engineering builds MVP.

---

## Launch

Release to selected cities.

---

## Growth

Expand to more cities.

---

## Maturity

Optimize performance and retention.

---

## Decline

Replace with a next-generation transportation platform if user behavior changes.

---

# The PM's Role Across the Lifecycle

| Stage       | PM Responsibility        |
| ----------- | ------------------------ |
| Discovery   | Understand problems      |
| Validation  | Test assumptions         |
| Planning    | Prioritize solutions     |
| Development | Guide execution          |
| Launch      | Measure adoption         |
| Growth      | Improve engagement       |
| Maturity    | Optimize value           |
| Retirement  | Make strategic decisions |

---

# Common Product Failures

## Failure #1

Building before understanding the problem.

---

## Failure #2

Skipping validation.

---

## Failure #3

Overbuilding the MVP.

---

## Failure #4

Ignoring post-launch metrics.

---

## Failure #5

Refusing to retire weak products.

---

# Interview Question Example

### Question

What is the most important stage in the Product Development Lifecycle?

### Strong Answer

All stages matter, but Discovery and Validation are often the most critical because building the wrong product perfectly is still a failure. Understanding customer problems and validating assumptions early reduces risk and prevents wasted development effort.

---

# Key Takeaways

1. Product development starts long before coding.
2. Discovery focuses on understanding problems.
3. Validation reduces product risk.
4. Planning defines the MVP and roadmap.
5. Launch is the beginning of learning.
6. Growth focuses on adoption and retention.
7. Mature products require optimization.
8. Every product eventually evolves or retires.
